Stacey de Voe's **The Spontaneous Dance Falls** drifts across histories of collective movement, lingering in the places where bodies in motion have unsettled authority and opened fleeting horizons of possibility. What do we lose when dance is contained, and what might be unleashed when we let it overflow?

The inquiry begins with the story of Frau Troffea and the 1518 “dancing plague” in Strasbourg, and stretches toward the recently abolished Swedish danstillstånd (1956-2023), a law that once demanded a permit to dance in public. Between these points runs a thread of unease: moments when dance, spontaneous or communal, became suspect, regulated, or forced back into containment. Rather than weaving these fragments into a continuous chronology, de Voe attends to the archive's silences and contradictions, letting a discontinuous but resonant history take shape.

Across centuries, dance surfaces in the record as both remedy and threat. In Sebastian Brant's *Ship of Fools* (1494), it appears as satanic temptation, while contemporary medical texts prescribe endless dancing as cure for mania. Such ambivalences recur: ritual dancing celebrated as healing yet dismissed as hysteria; social dances cherished as convivial gathering but feared as a danger to moral order. The Workers Dance League, bringing dance directly into union halls and working-class communities; American swing taxed and regulated during wartime; the Swedish Dansbaneeländet that both scandalised and electrified youth; rave collectives filling post-industrial cityscapes—each episode reveals how dancing bodies trouble the line between discipline joy, control and intimacy.

These histories echo in the present, where the body itself bears the weight of neoliberal demand. To dance is to test endurance: the folding of labour into life, the conversion of affect into productivity, the strain of fatigue tempered by sociability. A dance floor, then, is never just a place of leisure. It is a shifting ground where exhaustion rubs against persistence, where privatisation collides with collectivity, where spectacle turns into the rehearsal of political possibility.

de Voe stages these tensions in installations and performances that summon the ghosts of labour and festivity alike. Factories once humming, dance floors reverberating of past parties, glass shifting from molten pliancy to cold rigidity, mannequins poised in quiet suspension—such sites and materials stand as witnesses to what has been lost and what might yet be reclaimed. They speak of vanished spaces for gathering, but also of the chance to reimagine the commons through movement itself. Here, collectivity is not a dissolution of difference but a weaving of it, shaped in negotiation, friction, and the pulse of bodies together.

At skēnē, The Spontaneous Dance Falls continues from earlier chapters shown at Hägerstensåsens Medborgarhus in Stockholm and at Simian in Copenhagen. It extends de Voe's ongoing exploration of how dance can be read as archive, as spectacle, and as rehearsal for other ways of living together—a fragile yet insistent practice of world-making, where utopian possibility flickers in step, rhythm, and breath.

*Text by Karin Bähler Lavér*
